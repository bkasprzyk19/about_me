My Top Ten Folk Songs:

10. Thirty - The Weather Station
This song helped me turn 30. She drops an f-bomb out of nowhere and it forces you to listen to the song a second time to figure out why.
9. Home Again - Adam Carroll
Do you like the story of Odysseus? Adam Carroll tells it better than the Coen Brothers. He's the first, but not the last, Texan on this list.
8. Casey Jones - The Grateful Dead
Driving that train, high on cocaine, Casey Jones! You better watch your speed...
7. The Internationale - Woody Guthrie
It's the age of COVID. It's the age of protest. As folk protest songs go, this is one of the most fun. The Irish know how to write a ballad about revolution.
6. Look at Miss Ohio - Gillian Welch
Gillian Welch is one of my favorite songwriters of all time and this is one of her best. Another great driving song, especially in a classic convertible.
5. October - Jackson C. Frank
Jackson C. Frank has one of the most tragic histories of any folk singer. This song is haunting. One of those you don't really need to understand, in order to understand.
4. Lily, Rosemary, and the Jack of Hearts - Bob Dylan
Bob Dylan belongs on every top ten list of folk songs, it only comes down to which song. This is my favorite, a story of mystery and revenge. Also a song my dad would play on roadtrips.
3. Cover Me Up - Jason Isbell
Jason Isbell may be as close as any generation may come to having their own Bob Dylan. The album, Southeastern Son, is one of the rare albums you can listen to all the way through and never have to skip a song.
2. Dead Flowers - Townes Van Zandt
The second Texan on my list. Townes might be one of the coolest songwriters ever and his most beautiful song. This is actually a cover of a Rolling Stones song. Also, it's in one of the greatest movies of all time: The Big Lebowski.
1. 1952 Vincent Black Lightning - Richard Thompson
One of my favorite stories ever. An English song about a boy, a girl, a motorcycle, and armed robbery. With some technical but still pretty guitar playing as well for a background.
